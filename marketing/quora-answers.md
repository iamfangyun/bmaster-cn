回答模板 1
适用问题: "How much house can I afford?" / "How to calculate monthly mortgage payment?"
═══════════════════════════════════════════

Here's the thing most first-time buyers miss: your monthly payment is NOT just P&I (principal + interest).

Let me break it down with a real example.

Say you're buying a $400,000 home with 10% down at 7% APR, 30-year fixed:

**What most calculators show you:**
- P&I: $2,395/month ← This is what Bankrate and Zillow show by default

**What you'll ACTUALLY pay every month:**
- P&I: $2,395
- PMI (required below 20% down): $156
- Property tax (1.1% US average): $367
- Home insurance: $150
- **Total: $3,068/month**

That's $673 MORE than what the calculator told you. On a $5,000/month take-home, that's the difference between "comfortable" and "house poor."

**Three things nobody tells you:**

1. **PMI doesn't last forever.** Once your loan balance drops to 78% of the home value, PMI is automatically removed. On a $400K home with 10% down at 7%, that happens around year 10. After that, your payment drops by $156/month. Most calculators ignore this — they charge PMI for the full 30 years.

2. **Property tax varies wildly by state.** A $400K home in Hawaii costs $93/month in property tax. The same home in New Jersey costs $743/month. That's a $650/month difference for the exact same house.

3. **Closing costs add up.** Expect 2-5% of the home price in upfront costs: loan origination fee (1%), title insurance (0.5%), appraisal ($500-800), etc. On a $400K home, that's $8,000-20,000 in cash you need ON TOP of the down payment.

But honestly, even if you don't use it, just make sure whatever calculator you're using includes PMI, taxes, AND insurance in the monthly number. If it only shows P&I, it's lying to you.

回答模板 2
适用问题: "What is PMI and how do I get rid of it?" / "When does PMI go away?"
═══════════════════════════════════════════

PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance) is insurance YOU pay to protect the LENDER, not you. It's required when your down payment is less than 20%.

**How much does PMI cost?**

It depends on your down payment percentage:

| Down Payment | PMI Rate (annual) | On a $360K loan |
|---|---|---|
| 3-5% | 0.95% | $285/month |
| 5-10% | 0.78% | $234/month |
| 10-15% | 0.52% | $156/month |
| 15-20% | 0.37% | $111/month |
| 20%+ | 0% | $0 |

**When does PMI go away?**

By federal law (Homeowners Protection Act of 1998), PMI is automatically removed when your loan balance reaches 78% of the ORIGINAL home value — as long as you're current on payments.

For a typical 30-year loan at 7% with 10% down, that happens around year 10. Here's the key: after PMI drops, your payment goes DOWN, not stays the same.

Example: $400K home, 10% down, 7% APR:
- Years 1-10: $3,068/month (includes $156 PMI)
- Year 10+: $2,912/month (PMI removed)

You can also request PMI removal earlier if your home appreciates and you get a new appraisal showing 80% LTV.

**How to avoid PMI entirely:**
1. Put 20% down (obvious but hard)
2. Use a "piggyback loan" (80/10/10) — get a second loan for 10% to avoid PMI, but these have higher rates
3. Lender-paid PMI — lender pays PMI but charges you a higher interest rate (usually costs more over time)
4. VA loans (if you're a veteran) — no PMI ever

回答模板 3
适用问题: "Is it worth making extra mortgage payments?" / "Should I pay off my mortgage early?"
═══════════════════════════════════════════

Short answer: paying an extra $200/month on your mortgage is like getting a guaranteed 7% return on investment — because that's the interest rate you're avoiding.

Let me show you the math.

**Scenario: $360,000 loan, 7% APR, 30-year fixed**

Normal payment (no extra):
- Monthly P&I: $2,395
- Total interest over 30 years: $502,000
- Payoff: year 30

With extra $200/month:
- Monthly P&I: $2,595
- Total interest: $379,000
- Payoff: year 23.7
- **Interest saved: $123,000**
- **Years saved: 6.3 years**

That $123,000 in savings is a GUARANTEED return. You're not betting on the stock market — you're contractually avoiding interest you'd otherwise owe.

**When extra payments DON'T make sense:**

1. Your mortgage rate is below 4% — you'd likely earn more investing in index funds (historical ~8-10% return)
2. You haven't maxed out your 401(k) match — that's literally free money
3. You have higher-interest debt (credit cards at 20%+)
4. Your emergency fund is below 6 months

**The priority order for your money:**
1. Credit card debt (20%+)
2. 401(k) up to employer match (100% return)
3. Emergency fund (6 months)
4. Extra mortgage payments (guaranteed 7% return)
5. Index funds (8-10% but not guaranteed)
